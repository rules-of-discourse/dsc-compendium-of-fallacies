Appeal to Authority Fallacy

Fallacies

Basic Description

This isn't really a singar fallacy, but a category of fallacies - some of which are commonly referred to by the name of the category, others of which are not.

Appeal to Invalid Authority Fallacy

Generally, when one refers to a fallacy known as "Appeal to Authority", they are referring to what, for precision sake, should better be referred to as Appeal to Invalid Authority. This is when the word of a person of group is treated as an authoritative word on a given subject - when in reality that person or group has no special expertise on the relevant subject. Their lack of expertise on the relevant subject may be because the party in question has valid expertise, but on a substantially different subject - or it may be due to the party in question having no special expertise at all. All that matters, however, is not whether or not the party has expertise on a substantially different subject, but the mere fact that the party in question does not have special expertise on the particular subject relevant to the claim being made.

Appeal to Excessive Authority Fallacy

Not generally referred to to by this name, but possibly also under the "Appeal to Authority" header is another fallacy that should be referred to as the Appeal to Excessive Authority Fallacy. This is because the authority in question does indeed have special expertise in the subject at hand - however, the degree of confidence that is placed in what that authority says is excessive. One form of this fallacy might be treating the views of one valid expert in a relevant field with the level of confidence that is only properly given to the consensus of the community of experts as a whole.

Another form of this fallacy might be treating an uncertain prediction from a valid and relevant expert as though it were absolutely certain and infallible. One form of that fallacy (so well known that it has been the butt of many a joke) is treating a meteorologist's prediction of the weather as definitive and incapable of being wrong. Make no mistake, the weather predictions of a meteoroligist are a far cry more reliable than the weather predictions of a Ouija board - but it is common knowledge that even predictions labeled "100% chance" can be wrong.

This is not unique to meteorology, though, but to science in in general. Still -- it should be known that though the predictions of science are not infallbile, that is not an excuse to disregard science, nor is it an excuse to favor over science other explanations that by objective standards are far less reliable than science.

Distortion of Authority Fallacy

This fallacy is committed when the authority in question does indeed have valid special expertise on the subject - but the statement made by the authority is misrepresented and/or misinterpreted. A commission of this fallacy does not merely treat a statement as more definitive than it should properly be treated as coming from the authority in question, but actually misrepresents the contents of the statement itself.

One famous commentary on this fallacy is an editorial cartoon that came out some time during the late twentieth century in which a scientist is shown speaking to reporters announcing circumstantial evidence that there may have been life on Mars a very long time ago. The reporters respond with glee, going bezerk over how we are not alone, but have neighbors on Mars that may come and visit us at any time. Below it all, the caption reads: "Let us hope that life on Mars is more intelligent than life on Earth."

Of course, there is no known case in which the Distortion of Authority Fallacy is liable to be referred to as "Appeal to Authority" - however, it's relation to fallacies that might be is such that it is appropriate to include it in that family of fallacies.

Razors

Step 1: START HERE
Does the authority making the claim have valid special expertise in the area of knowledge that covers the claim?
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to step 2.
If the answer is "no"
This is an instance of the Appeal to Invalid Authority Fallacy.
Step 2
Is the claim that is made by the authority in question being accurately represented and interpreted?
This includes expressions of uncertainty contained in the wording of the claim itslef, unless those expressions are mere acknowledgements of the given authority's own inherent limitations.
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to step 3.
If the answer is "no"
This is an instance of the Distortion of Authority Fallacy.
Step 3
Is the claim in question being treated with greater confidence than it ought to be given the limitations of the authority that made the claim?
If any expressions of uncertainty in the wording of the claim itself are indeed mere acknowledgements of tha authority's own limitations, this is the place to take them into account. However, absence of such wording in the claim does not confer any level of infallibility on the claimant.
If the answer is "yes"
This is an instance of the Appeal to Excessive Authority Fallacy.
If the answer is "no"
You are in the clear. None of these fallacies are being committed on this count. This appeal to authority is in fact a cogent one.
