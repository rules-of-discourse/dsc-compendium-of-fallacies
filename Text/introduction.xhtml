Introduction

The Discursive Compendium of Fallacies and Malconducts is the heart and soul of the Rules of Discourse projects. The other documents put out by the early efforts of the project, which focused on procedural guidelines, have been demonstrated by testing to be of lesser importance than the Compendium, and may even be phased out entirely in the near future.

As work began in the Rules of Discourse project, it became clear very early on that a compendium of fallacies would be needed. As the project of building a Compendium of Fallacies moved on, it became eventually clear that fallacies were only one of four categories that needed to be covered in this compendium. However, it's title remained "Discursive Compendium of Fallacies" until 2018-03-19, when it's name was changed to "Discursive Compendium of Fallacies and Malconducts".

The other three categories are given names that are either partially or fully coined in the development of this compendium and of the Rules of Discourse. These categories are cogencies, malconducts, and bonconducts.

A cogency is the opposite of a fallacy. Both fallacies and cogencies are patterns of reasoning - but fallacies are flawed patterns, while cogencies are valid, logically sound patterns of reasoning.

A malconduct is a pattern of behavior that is unconducive to proper discourse. A malconduct differs from a fallacy in that a fallacy is a pattern of reasoning, while a malconduct is a pattern of behavior. Adding a category to the compendium for malconducts was deemed necessary because, historically, some malconducts (such as Appeal to Force) were mis-labeled as fallacies for lack of a malconduct category - while other malconcuts (such as the Homework Malconduct) never were properly documented at all.

In French, "mal" is the term for "bad", and "bon" is the term for "good". Therefore, I replaced the prefix "mal" in "malconduct" with the prefix "bon" to form the term "bonconduct". As you may guess, a bonconduct relates to a malconduct the same way that a cogency relates to a fallacy.

Why Write a New Compendium?

While the Discursive Compendium of Fallacies and Malconducts is the official compendium for use with the Rules of Discourse, there is no requirement (or even any suggestion) to use it exclusively. There are several other compendia out there which are very useful.

That said, while existing compendia are useful, they leave much to be desired. Their taxonomy is a hoj-poj with little or no rhyme or reason. Redundancies abound - or at least many obscure fallacies are listed in ways that make it difficult to distinguish them from other, similar fallacies. What's worse - often they aren't sufficiently explicit about how to resolve disputes about whether or not a particular fallacy has been committed in the first place.

It is for this reason that a priority will be made, for each entry in this compendium, to develop and include a set of razors - questions and tests for resolving the distinction between potentially confusing patterns of reason or patterns of behavior --- distinctions that can potentially make the difference between a cogency and a fallacy, or between a bonconduct and a malconduct. At very least, it can help put the appropriate label on a flawed pattern.

This compendium is still in it's early stages of development. However, hopefully, when it gets to be a bit more complete, it will address these shortcomings.
