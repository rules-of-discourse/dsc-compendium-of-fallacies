Crying Wolf Malconduct

Malconducts

Basic Description

The Crying Wolf Malconduct is the malconduct that is committed by someone when zie wrongfully levies an accusation of a fallacy or a malconduct having been committed.

Crying Wolf is a very harmful malconduct. When someone Cries Wolf, zie squanders the resources of the group or zir discussion partner in the effort to refute the bogus assertion. Risk is in incurred that a solid argument might be held in wrongful doubt or even be wrongfully struck down. Furthermore, the one who Cries Wolf damages zir credibility, which can have other adverse consequences on when zir credibility is needed - such as if zie has occasion to call attention to a true instance of a fallacy or malconduct being committed.

Quite possibly the most common form of this malconduct is an accusation of the Ad Hominem fallacy in situations where the criterion for any form of Argumentum ad Homenim haven't been met, let alone the fallacious form thereof.

Origin of the Name

This malconduct derives its name from a famous Aesop fable about a shepherd boy who, either craving attention or wishing to commit a prank, cries out that a wolf is attacking his flock even though in reality no such thing is happening. The villagers come armed to eliminate the threat to the shepherd boy's flock and are enraged at having been summoned for naught. After a few times of this, they cease to take the shepherd boy's cries seriously.

It is after the villagers have ceased to heed the shepherd boy's cries that a wolf finally shows up in reality. Upon detecting the wolf, the shepherd boy cries out to the villagers for help - but when they hear his cries, their prior experience with him causes them to assume that he is merely playing again the same prank that he had played multiple times before. As a result, the wolf is left unchecked to devour the shepherd boy's entire flock and, according to some versions, also the shepherd boy himself.

Precautions

The first thing to do to avoid committing the Crying Wolf malconduct is to make sure that you have a solid understanding of the meaning of any fallacy or malconduct that you accuse someone of. Be sure that all of the criteria of the impropriety in question are met before you levy the accusation.

Remember that logic and reason are not subjective. You are looking for something that is very clear cut at meeting all criteria of the impropriety in question. If it merely is something that can possibly be construed as meeting all criteria, then there is a good chance that you're unconsciously fishing to discredit something because it is inconvenient, not because of an actual fallacy or malconduct.

Also, be aware that all of us, you included, harbor within us an inclination to discredit actions and arguments that are inconvenient to us or to our agenda whether they deserve such discrediting or not. Examine your motives to be certain that such a bias isn't behind the accusation that you're considering. Bear in mind that if necessary, the need for such self-examination can be valid grounds for a processing pause request.
