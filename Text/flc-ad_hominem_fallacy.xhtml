Ad Hominem Fallacy

Fallacies

General Description

The ad hominem is committed by Person B when Person A makes an argument or claim and Person B moves to discredit the argument by citing a personal characterisitic of Person A that isn't relevant to Person A's qualification to make that argument or claim.

Though generally, it is the policy of this compendium to prefer English names over Latin names, the term "ad hominem" has been used sufficiently in English context to qualify as a loan word - especially in light of the fact that there exists no recognizable term that is more firmly English to refer to this particular fallacy (and to it's corresponding cogency of the same name).

Razors

For this razor sequence, Person B is the one suspected of committing the ad hominem fallacy, and Person A is the recipient of the suspectedly fallacious argument. Argument C is to refer to the argument made by Person A.

Step 1 - Start here
Is the reference to a personal characteristic of Person A at all in the first place part of the basis that Person B proposes for discrediting Argument C?
If the answer is "yes"
Okay - then it is an ad hominem argument - but not necessarily a fallacious one. There is in fact a such thing as the ad hominem cogency. To distinguish which category this argument falls into, proceed to Step 2.
If the answer is "no"
This isn't an ad hominem argument at all - let alone a fallacious form thereof. Believe it or not, there are cases that wind up here that were accused of the ad hominem fallacy - but in those cases, the accusation was unwarranted.
Step 2
Does Argument C hinge on any claim that the recipient is asked to accept on the authority of Person A?
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to Step 3.
If the answer is "no"
Then this clearly is a case of the ad-hominem fallacy. If Person A posits nothing on zir own authority, then the argument must stand or fall on it's own merit, and any attack based on personal characteristics of Person A are irrelevant.
Step 3
Is the characteristic of Person A that is referenced to discredit Argument C relevant to Person A's authority and/or trustworthiness to make whatever claims in the argument rest on Person A's own authority?
If the answer is "yes"
No - this isn't a case of the ad hominem fallacy. Rather, it is a case of the ad hominem cogency, which is a valid pattern of reasoning.
If the answer is "no"
Then this is indeed a case of the ad hominem fallacy.
