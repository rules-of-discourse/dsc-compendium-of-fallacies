Argument from Ignorance Fallacy

Fallacies

Basic Description

The Argument from Ignorance Fallacy is when one proposition is accepted as true based too heavily on the mere premise that its negation hasn't been proven.

This pattern of reasoning is flawed because if neither a proposition nor its negation have been proven, the reality is that don't know which one is true and which one is false.

Razors

Step 1
Is the person suspected of committing the fallacy Person A asserting that one position Proposition B is true based on the premise that it's negation Proposition C hasn't been proven?
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to Step 1.5
If the answer is "no"
In the clear. This argument doesn't even have the most rudimentary form in common with the fallacy in question.
Step 1.5
Is the argument based on the assertion that the nature Proposition C is such that, were it true, proof would be forthcoming?
If the answer is "yes"
Further Razors Pending. There is indeed a valid form of argumentation along such lines. However, it must be examined whether or not this argument meets the requirement for that form of argumentation. When the entry on that form of argumentation is ready, a link will be added here to that entry so that the razor-scheme in that entry can be followed.
If the answer is "no"
Proceed to Step 2
Step 2
Is there actual foundation to support Proposition B?
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to Step 3
If the answer is "no"
Guilty as charged. As a matter of fact, this is probably the most cut-and-dry variant of this fallacy possible.
Step 3
Has the Proposition B's foundation Foundation D been properly established?
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to Step 4
If the answer is "no"
Guilty as charged. This is indeed an instance of the fallcy in question.
Step 4
Has the validity of the argument leading from Foundation D to Proposition B Argument E been properly verified?
If the answer is "yes"
Proceed to Step 5
If the answer is "no"
Guilty as charged. Until Argument E has been properly verified, Proposition B can not be accepted as true. Jumping that gun simply because Proposition C hasn't been proven either definitely counts as an invocation of this fallacy.
Step 5
Does Argument E, upon verification, qualify as conclusive proof of Proposition B or merely a case in favor of Proposition B?
If it constitutes conclusive proof
Proceed to Step 8
If it merely constitutes a case in favor
Proceed to Step 6
Step 6
Is there a counter-case in favor of Proposition C?
If the answer is "yes"
Guilty as charged. Both Propositions have arguments in their favor - but neither is proven. To treat one argument as conclusive simply because the other isn't is definitely the kind of double-standard that this fallacy label covers.
If the answer is "no"
Proceed to Step 7
Step 7
Is the lack of proof of Proposition C used as an excuse to express a greater degree of confidence in Proposition B than Argument E warrants?
If the answer is "yes"
Guilty as charged. Yes - it is to a lesser degree, but still guilty.
If the answer is "no"
In the clear.
Step 8
So it seems that you have conclusive proof of Proposition B. This means that, hypothetically, if you also appeared to have conclusive proof of Proposition C that there would have to have been some flaw in proof (either the proof of Proposition B, the proof of Proposition C, or both proofs) that somehow slipped through all previous efforts at scrutiny.
Such a sanity-check can not serve as a substitute for scrutinizing proofs as well as humanly possible. However, if scrutiny has been done with all due vigilance, such a sanity-check may be done as one additional measure.
Is the sanity-check here described the reason for noting that Proposition C hasn't been proven?
If the answer is "yes"
In the clear - provided, of course, that this sanity-check is in addition to other due vigilance in scrutinizing proofs and not instead of it.
If the answer is "no"
Guilty as charged. The fallacy has indeed been committed - and the fact that the fallacy so-happens to lead to a true conclusion doesn't change that.
